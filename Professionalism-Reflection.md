# Professionalism Refelection

## Reflection

**Have you ever experienced or witnessed great teamwork and professionalism? What made it effective?**

    Great professionalism is effective when it is action-oriented. I’ve seen this work best when teammates take ownership of their tasks and don't wait for permission to explore solutions. This self-led mindset keeps projects moving and reduces the need for micromanagement. I've also experienced great teamwork when all members have a clear understanding of their designated tasks and can ask other members questions whenever they are stuck, ensuring that no one is left struggling in isolation.

**What steps can you take to make sure your communication is clear, respectful, and inclusive?**

    - Eliminating Ambiguity: I will avoid misleading language. My communication will be literal and direct, ensuring that my intent is transparent to all teammates, especially those who are neurodivergent and rely on factual clarity.

    - Structured Updates: I will provide progress reports using bullet points and clear headings to make my work easy to understand at a glance.

**How can you help create a positive, fun, and professional work environment?**

    I can contribute to the work environment by being a reliable and low-friction teammate. I believe a fun workplace is one where the work is organized, allowing everyone to feel relaxed and supportive. I will engage in creative brainstorming with an open mind while maintaining high accountability for my deliverables.

**How can you prepare effectively for meetings with supervisors and colleagues?**

    - I will integrate a 30-minute rule to protect the team's deep work time and build my own problem-solving skills. When I encounter a technical problem, I will spend 30 minutes utilizing external resources to find a solution. If I am still stuck after 30 minutes, I will reach out. This ensures I am not really wasting hours, but also demonstrates to my supervisor that I have done the "homework" and can present exactly where I got stuck. This makes the eventual help I receive much more specific and efficient.

    - I will enter every call with a written list of three things: what I’ve achieved since we last spoke, the specific "blockers" stopping me from moving faster, and questions that require supervisor input. ather than waiting for a "status report" prompt, I will take the initiative to present my updates. This respects my supervisor's time by keeping the meeting focused and ensures that we address the most critical items first.

**What would it look like to be proactive during your internship?**

I will apply the Recommendation-First mindset to shift from being a passive task-taker to a proactive designer. Instead of asking open-ended questions like 'What do I do now?', which puts the burden of thinking on the supervisor, I will provide a clear path forward. I will use a specific formula for updates (Status + Proposed Action + Validation) to demonstrate that I am thinking ahead and fully understand the project’s goals.

**How might you follow up with colleagues or supervisors if you need information/action from them? How would you escalate it further if you don't get a response and when would you escalate?**

    I will follow the established urgency ladder to respect team focus:

    - Low Urgency: Email (48-hour response window).

    - Medium Urgency: Discord/Teams (24-hour response window).

    - High Urgency: SMS (2-hour response window).

    - Emergency: Phone Call.

**What are some things you won't do?**

    - I will not sit idle: If I finish a task, I will look for ways to improve existing designs or check in with teammates to see where I can assist.

    - I will not ignore feedback: I will treat every critique as a learning opportunity and ensure I don't repeat the same mistake in future iterations.

    - I will not wing it: I will not attend meetings or start tasks without first reviewing the relevant project briefs and documentation.
