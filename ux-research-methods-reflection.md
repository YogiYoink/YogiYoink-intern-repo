# UX Research Methods Reflection

## Reflection

**If Focus Bear wanted to improve its onboarding experience, which UX research methods would be most useful?**

    The most useful would be Usability Testing and User Interviews. Watching a new user try to set up their first routine would immediately show us where they get confused. Following that with an interview would help us understand if the startup cost of the app feels worth it to them. We could also use A/B Testing to try out different onboarding flows and see which one leads to better user retention. Additionally, surveys could be helpful to gather quantitative data on user satisfaction with the onboarding process.

**What are the "jobs to be done" for Focus Bear users? (include the different categories of jobs not just the obvious ones)**

    I think of it this way: people don’t just use Focus Bear to track time, they hire it to save their day from slipping away. For a student or a developer, the Functional Job is having an automated deep work mode that silences the noise of social media so they can actually solve a complex problem without losing their train of thought every five minutes. On an Emotional level, the app is hired to stop that imposter syndrome spiral. It turns the anxiety of a looming deadline into a sense of calm confidence because the system is doing the heavy lifting of staying disciplined. Socially, it’s about the win of going into a meeting or a study group and being the person who actually hit their goals, rather than the one making excuses. Even the Supporting Jobs matter, like a gentle nudge to grab some water or look away from the screen because it prevents that zombie feeling at the end of a long session.

**What challenges might arise when collecting self-reported user feedback?**

    The biggest challenge is Social Desirability Bias. Users often tell you what they think you want to hear, or they might genuinely misremember how they used the app. This is why we should balance what they say in an interview with what we see in a usability test. For example, a user might say they love a feature in an interview, but when we watch them use the app, we might see that they struggle with it or avoid it altogether. This inconsistency can make it difficult to get an accurate understanding of user behavior and preferences. To mitigate this, we should use a combination of research methods to gather both qualitative and quantitative data, ensuring that we have a well-rounded view of the user experience.

**How can UX designers avoid bias when conducting research?**

    We avoid bias by asking neutral, open-ended questions and avoiding leading the user. Instead of saying, "Do you like this simple layout?", we ask, "What are your thoughts on the screen layout?" We also try to test with a diverse group of neurodivergent users to ensure we aren't just designing for one specific type of minds. It's important to create an inclusive research environment where all users feel comfortable sharing their honest feedback.
