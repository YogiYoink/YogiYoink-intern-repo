# UX Collaboration Reflection

## Reflection

**How can UX ensure a smooth handoff between design and development?**

    The best way to ensure a smooth handoff is to over-communicate. Instead of just sending a Figma link, I should organize the file with clear flowcharts, edge cases (like what happens if there’s no internet connection), and "Redlines" that explain spacing and behavior. A quick walkthrough meeting before they start coding can save hours of back-and-forth later. I should also be open to questions and feedback from the developers, and be ready to clarify any design decisions or make adjustments if needed. The goal is to work together to create the best possible user experience, so collaboration and communication are key.

**What challenges might arise when design, dev, and product teams have conflicting priorities?**

    A common challenge that I found while learning more about collaboration is the "Triangle of Constraints." It is when Design wants it perfect, Product wants it fast, and Engineering wants it stable. This can lead to tension if the design is too complex for the current deadline. To avoid this, we have to find a middle ground like starting with a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) that is functional and user-friendly, then adding the "fancy" features in later updates. It’s important to have open discussions about what’s essential for the user experience and what can be iterated on later. Prioritizing user needs while also considering technical feasibility and time constraints can help align the teams and create a product that satisfies everyone.

**If a developer modifies a UX design due to technical limitations, how should a UX designer respond?**

    Instead of being protective of the original design, a UX designer should treat it as a collaborative problem-solving moment. I’d ask "What specifically is the technical hurdle?", and once I understand the limitation, I can design an alternative that still solves the user's problem but is easier to build. It’s better to have a slightly different, working design than a perfect one that never gets shipped. I should also be open to the idea that the developer might have a creative solution that I hadn’t thought of. The key is to keep the user’s needs at the center of the conversation and work together to find a solution that meets those needs while also being technically feasible. This way, we can maintain a positive working relationship and create a product that delivers value to our users.
