## Reflection

**How can low-fidelity wireframes help avoid costly design mistakes?**

    They catch structural flaws early. For example, if we realize during the wireframe stage that a button is hidden under three menus, we can fix it in seconds. If we wait until the feature is coded to realize it's hard to use, we’ve wasted hours of engineering time. Wireframes are the blueprints before the house is built. They allow us to test the user flow and layout without getting distracted by colors and fonts. It’s much cheaper to move a box around in a wireframe than to rewrite code after development.

**If a stakeholder wants to jump straight into high-fidelity design, how would you explain the value of low-fi wireframing?**

    I would explain that skipping to high-fidelity is like picking out the paint colors for a house before you've decided where the front door goes. High-fi designs often trigger feedback on subjective things like colors or icons, whereas low-fi ensures the logic works. By starting low-fi, we ensure the foundation of the user experience is solid before we invest time in the aesthetic details. It also allows for faster iteration. If we need to change the layout, it’s much easier to do in a wireframe than in a polished design. I’d also mention that low-fi wireframes can be more inclusive for non-designers, as they focus on functionality rather than visual design, making it easier for everyone to provide feedback on the core user experience.

**What are some common mistakes designers make when creating wireframes?**

    One common mistake is adding too much detail, which can distract from the purpose of the wireframe. For example, including specific images or exact text can lead stakeholders to focus on those elements rather than the overall layout and user flow. Another mistake is not involving the development team early enough. If designers create wireframes in isolation, they might design something that’s technically difficult to implement, leading to frustration later on. Additionally, some designers forget to test their wireframes with real users, which can result in a design that looks good on paper but doesn’t meet user needs in practice. It’s important to keep wireframes simple and focused on functionality while also collaborating closely with developers and users throughout the process.
