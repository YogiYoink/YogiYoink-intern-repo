# UX Research Impact Reflection

## Reflection

**If research contradicts a designer’s personal preference, what should happen?**

    The data must win. As a designer, I might love a minimalist and hidden menu because it looks clean, but if research shows that neurodivergent users can't find it, I have to abandon my preference. Our job is to solve problems for the user, not to satisfy our own aesthetic tastes. It's about putting the user's needs above our own preferences. If the research indicates that a different design would better serve the users, we need to be willing to adapt and change our designs accordingly.

**How can UX designers ensure that their work aligns with real user needs?**

    The best way is to keep the User Personas front and center. I should constantly ask: "Does this help a student with time blindness, or am I just making it look pretty?" Regularly jumping into user interviews and looking at real feedback keeps us grounded in reality. It's about maintaining a user-centered mindset throughout the design process. By continuously engaging with users and referring back to their needs and pain points, we can ensure that our designs are not just aesthetically pleasing but also functional and effective in solving real problems.

**What risks come with relying on assumptions rather than research when designing an interface?**

    Assumptions lead to designer bias. We assume everyone uses technology the way we do, but neurodivergent users often have very different sensory and cognitive boundaries. Designing on assumptions often results in friction that causes users to quit the app because it feels too difficult or stressful to navigate. This can lead to a loss of users and a failure to meet the needs of a significant portion of the user base. It can also damage the brand's reputation if users feel that their needs are not being considered or met. Relying on assumptions can create barriers for users, especially those with disabilities, and can ultimately lead to a product that is not inclusive or accessible.
